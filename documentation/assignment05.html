Overview

This project explores sound-driven visual experiments using fragment shaders in Shadertoy. Instead of drawing shapes sequentially, each shader treats sound as data that influences every pixel simultaneously, mapping audio waveforms and FFT frequency information to color, movement, and distortion across the screen.

Through a series of 15–20 images, the project investigates different visual translations of sound—such as oscilloscope lines, spectrum bars, circular waves, and ripple-like surfaces—using shader logic to explore sound as vibration, rhythm, and spatial force. The work connects shader-based graphics with my thesis research on sound and material behavior, treating sound not as an overlay but as a generative structure.

Concept

Sound is treated not as an event over time, but as a field of vibration distributed across space. By using fragment shaders—where the same instruction runs simultaneously on every pixel—this project aligns sound’s continuous, wave-based nature with the parallel logic of GPU computation. Each image becomes a moment where sound modulates space, turning frequency, amplitude, and rhythm into visual tension, resonance, and flow rather than representation.
